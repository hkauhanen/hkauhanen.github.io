With an average 12 kg per annum per capita consumption, Finns are known to take their coffee very seriously. My favourite method of preparation is the espresso, which I’ve been hooked on from about 2005, and which I’ve been making myself since 2019. Espresso is endlessly fascinating: it can be delicious and surprising, but also capricious and frustrating. Here’s what I’ve learned so far.

Life is too short for bad espresso

Your grinder really is that important

It’s often said that your grinder is as, if not more, important than your espresso machine. This seems to be true: extremely fine variations in grind size can lengthen an espresso shot by seconds, and many low-end grinders either lack the accuracy to dial in a desired grind setting or the precision to produce a grind distribution that is sufficiently peaked about the expectation. If you ever have a chance to compare two grinders back-to-back, fixing all other variables (espresso machine, beans, brew parameters), the experience can be quite revealing. I had this opportunity when upgrading from a Graef CM 800 (an entry-level conical burr grinder, ~125€ at the time of writing) to a Eureka Mignon Silenzio (an entry-level but ‘proper’ espresso grinder with flat 50mm burrs, ~400€). Isolating other variables, I pulled a shot first with coffee ground with the Graef, then with coffee ground with the Eureka. The Graef cup was a confusing mix of sour and bitter notes and left an unwelcome aftertaste. The Eureka cup was smooth and balanced. For me, this was an eye-opener as I had believed the Graef to be capable of producing good espresso: it could certainly grind fine enough to choke my machine. But, as I discovered, the grind size distribution plays a key role and is what likely contributed to my perception of both sour and bitter notes in the cup simultaneously.

Other considerations when choosing a grinder include the amount of ‘dead space’ inside (due to its design the Graef, for instance, retains several grams of coffee inside, leading to significant coffee wastage if fresh grounds are desired), noise levels (the Eureka is, according to my introspective and entirely unscientific impression, about half as loud as the Graef), and ease of cleaning.

(The above may seem unnecessarily harsh on the Graef. However, in my experience, it just doesn’t cut it for espresso, and one ought to be wary of grinders in this price range in general. I’d have no qualms about using the Graef for drip, or in a pressurized portafilter, however.)

Avoid roasting extremes

Don’t overdose

A modern trend is to use a lot of ground coffee per extracted end product. This is not necessarily good. As Giorgio Milos points out,

The biggest mistake I’ve seen is an enormous quantity of coffee being used—way too much. I’m talking about 20 to 25 grams of coffee for a single espresso shot! It is like making a mojito with half a mint leaf, one ice cube, a few grains of sugar, and a gallon of rum. Undrinkable!

Instead of blindly following what’s trendy, I’d strongly recommend experimenting to find the brew ratio that works with your coffee and for your taste buds. For me and the medium-roasted ~80/20 blends that I normally work with, that’s 14 grams in (in a double basket), about 40 grams out (20 g/cup, or roughly 25 ml/cup), for a brew ratio of 0.35, extracted at 9 bar in 25 seconds (from the moment the pump is engaged, not from first drip, which usually occurs at around 6–7 seconds). This could be considered low by modern standards, but it works for me.

Don’t expect espresso to behave linearly

Be mindful of the properties of your water

Keep it clean

Cleanliness is godliness in all coffee brewing methods, but especially so with espresso, where the tiniest imperfections of procedure can make the difference between a delicious cup and one that goes down the sink. The importance of keeping your equipment clean cannot be overemphasized. Also do not be fooled into thinking (as I was for a long time) that a fixed amount of cleaning effort suffices to remove any amount of built up gunk. The losses are superlinear: the longer you neglect to properly maintain your equipment, the more difficult the chore becomes. The problem is exacerbated by the fact that coffee oils are capable of producing some rather interesting aromas as they go rancid… I learned this the hard way when trying to remove the smell of pea soup (sic) from the shower screen of my first machine and the smell of smoked fish (sic) from my first grinder.

Here’s my current cleaning schedule, sufficient for my current espresso preparation frequency (1–2 pulls on weekdays, 3–4 on Saturday and Sunday each). Water in my area is on the hard side (FIXME units).

Daily

  • Flush shower screen
  • Clean portafilter and basket(s) with water (or small amount of washing up liquid if especially dirty)
  • Empty drip tray
  • Wipe outside of machine with a microfibre cloth (no detergents)

Weekly

  • Backflush with water
  • Refresh boiler water by drawing one boilerful of water from the steam wand
  • Grinder: Remove bean hopper and beans from hopper, wipe with a microfibre cloth. Clean grinding chamber and dispensing lip with a brush.

Every 2 months

  • Backflush with brewing group cleaner

Every 3 months

  • Prophylactic descaling

Be fair

Espresso, even more than other forms of coffee consumption, is systemically unequal: it is a way of preparing coffee that those who grow, pick and process the coffee could rarely afford. Coffee production continues to be highly problematic on a global scale, and it isn’t too far-fetched to call the consumption of the very cheapest varieties of coffee an advanced form of colonialism. Please do not support practices that exploit those at the early links of the coffee chain. Instead, support initiatives that guarantee sustainable minimal wages and working conditions (and also, importantly, proper care for the environment).

In espresso, the challenge at the moment is that very few fairtrade and organic coffees are available, and those that are available tend to fill a particular niche of the flavour space, typically identified with third wave espresso. For those of us who like a traditional Italian blend and roast, there isn’t much to choose from, unfortunately. I can recommend FIXME, however.
