How to get the best out of your Gran Gaggia

crumb / 15 March 2020
#coffee

“A mathematician is a device for turning coffee into theorems.” This statement by Alfréd Rényi (often misattributed to Erdős) can be adapted, I think, to describe the character of other kinds of scientists, too. Hence a post such as this may not be as off-topic on an academic webpage as might at first seem.

This post, in particular, describes my experience chasing the Holy Grail of the perfect espresso using the budget Gran Gaggia espresso maker. Designed by Italian Gaggia, the machine is made in China and promises to make good coffee for a very reasonable price: I believe I paid around 80 euros on Amazon (shame on me…) for my (new) unit thanks to a discount scheme, though a more usual retail price would hover around 150 euros I believe. I have the “Deluxe” model, which means a stainless steel front panel in an otherwise plastic housing. (The Deluxe sits squarely in between the less superlatively named all-plastic “Style” and the upmarket stainless steel “Prestige”.) What I say here about the Deluxe applies to the other models just as well.

I must begin with a somewhat pessimistic note: if you are passionate about espresso, and are planning to buy the Gran Gaggia, then don’t. While the price tag will be alluring when compared against what you’d pay for more professional machines, and while the Gran Gaggia is definitely able to make a very decent cup if properly treated, in the long run the limitations of the machine will get you. Espresso is an incredibly finicky drink, with a parameter space of 9+ dimensions (and those involved in Espresso 2.0 can add more things like pre-infusion and coffee growing altitude to the mix): the beans, roast level, grind size, quantity of grounds, tamping force, water quantity, water quality, extraction time and the position of the stars all have an effect on the final product. Espresso, in fact, would not be a bad example to illustrate the notion of bifurcation: very small continuous variations in any of the above parameters can make the difference between a mouth-orgasm and a cup that belongs in the sink.

But if, like me, you already bought the machine and would like to hear how I’ve learnt to negotiate its quirks, read on.

Tip 1: DON’T neglect the importance of the grinder

If you do not have a burr grinder yet, get one. It is often said that, in espresso, the grinder has more of an influence on the resulting cup of coffee than the espresso maker itself. While this may be overstating it a bit, it is important to have access to a grinder that can (a) grind to the rather fine sizes required by espresso and (b) do this consistently, so that the variance in grain size is kept to a minimum. Since grind size is such an important control parameter in espresso-making, it is simply not enough to rely on pre-ground coffee.

True to my stingy nature, I am using a Graef CM800: this grinder sits at the lower (and cheaper) end of grinders that are capable of espresso. In practice, I have found it to be more than sufficient, though it may be necessary to fine-tune the placement of the upper burr to enable the finer grind sizes (see the Graef’s excellent manual for instructions on how to do this). The build quality of the CM800 is also good: with its aluminium housing and airplane-cockpit-flip-switch-esque on/off switch it is definitely sturdier and more handsome than my Gran Gaggia.

Tip 2: DO de-pressurize the supplied portafilter

The Gran Gaggia is, in fact, supposed to be used with pre-ground coffee. In an effort to remove one variable from the equation for the large customer base that is likely to buy this machine, Gaggia supply the coffee maker with a “Crema Perfetta” filter holder. This is a so-called pressurized portafilter: there is a valve, implemented through the use of a simple spring, that only opens when a nominal pressure is reached. This means that an espresso-like drink can be achieved with coffee ground to not-quite espresso fineness, or with less than fresh coffee. The problem? In a proper espresso, the coffee itself – not a valve – is what provides the required resistance to build up the optimal extraction pressure. A pressurized filter simply will not give the same taste, mouthfeel or consistency in the final product; in fact, the crema (even if perfect in name) will look very different.

Fortunately, it is very easy to “de-pressurize” the Gaggia filter: unscrew the three screws on the underside of the portafilter, take out all plastic parts and the pressurizing spring, and reattach the black plastic handle to the aluminium basket (now free of the pressurizing system) with the three screws. Put the pressurizer mechanism in a safe place in case you ever want to pressurize the portafilter again.

Postscript

One could say that I am deliberately mis-using my Gran Gaggia by running it with a de-pressurized portafilter and expecting it to perform things it wasn’t designed to perform. Is it worth it?

As I hinted at in the beginning, if you are serious about espresso it may be best to pass over this machine – and others in its class – and bite the bullet and get a Gaggia Classic or Rancilio Silvia or FIXME right from the get-go.
